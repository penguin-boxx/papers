This technique is important for garbage-collector-free stack allocation, ownership and borrowing, and capability-based effect handlers, where the capability used to invoke an operation must not outlive the handler that grants it. \section{Introduction}
Escape analysis determines whether a value can outlive its lexical scope. It originated as a compiler optimization, for example for stack allocation and the elimination of intermediate objects~\cite{park1992escape,blanchet1999escape}. In modern languages this property increasingly becomes part of the type discipline: Rust ties it to lifetimes and borrowing~\cite{matsakis2014rust}, OCaml develops a closely related idea through modal control of locality~\cite{lorenzen2024oxidizing}, and Kotlin uses contracts for restricted cases of non-local control~\cite{akhin2021kotlin}. Verification of such mechanisms has likewise become a topic in its own right: RustBelt provides a semantic foundation for the safety of Rust~\cite{jung2018rustbelt,jung2020stacked}, while Prusti, Creusot, RustHornBelt, and RefinedRust show that properties of Rust programs can be lifted to the level of machine-checked specifications~\cite{astrauskas2022prusti,denis2022creusot,matsushita2022rusthornbelt,gaher2024refinedrust}.

A separate reason to make escape analysis type-based is connected with algebraic effect handlers. In capability-based systems an effect operation is invoked through a capability value; if such a capability leaves the dynamic extent of its handler, the program gains access to a handler that no longer exists. The languages and calculi Effekt, capture tracking, and evidence-passing handlers address this problem by different means~\cite{brachthauser2020effects,brachthauser2022effects,boruch2023capturing,xie2021generalized,xie2022first}. Existing approaches, however, usually require either explicit capture sets or a heavy polymorphic infrastructure. The $Core_\Delta$ calculus~\cite{stoyan2025existential} offers a different trade-off: lifetimes form a small lattice $free \sqsubseteq local$ with an explicit minimum operation, and compound data types hide the lifetimes of their fields existentially and recover them on pattern matching.

The difficulty is that such a combination is convenient for the programmer but non-trivial for the metatheory. Existential lifetimes interact with substitution, subtyping, and variance; effect handlers add continuation capture and runtime markers. A pen-and-paper proof can easily miss a case in which a local value or a capability ends up in the result of a function. The goal of this work is to give a machine-checked metatheory of an extended $Core_\Delta$ with effect handlers and to show which guarantees of escape analysis actually follow from the typing rules.

The main contributions of the paper are the following. First, $Core_\Delta$ is extended with single-operation effect handlers in a capability-passing style. Second, a small-step semantics is defined with marker-indexed evaluation contexts and runtime values for continuations. Third, the syntax, semantics, subtyping, and typing are mechanized in Rocq/Coq. Fourth, progress, preservation, and type safety are proved modulo four standard substitution lemmas for the de Bruijn representation. Fifth, two domain-specific guarantees are formalized: a capability does not escape its handler, and a value observed at a type with lifetime $free$ does not carry a top-level $local$.

\section{Related Work and Problem Statement}\label{sec:related-work}
Classical region systems made lifetime an explicit object of typing long before modern ownership languages~\cite{tofte1997region,fluet2006monadic}. Their goal is close to ours: to replace a global analysis with local conditions at function boundaries. The difference is that $Core_\Delta$ does not parameterize every user-defined type by a lifetime. A data constructor computes the minimum of the lifetimes of its arguments, and pattern matching introduces fresh existential variables bounded by the lifetime of the scrutinized value. This reduces the number of annotations but complicates the proof of type preservation under reduction.

Effect handlers have been studied from both the denotational and the operational side~\cite{plotkin2013handling,zyuzin2021contextual,van2022handling,van2024framework,tang2025modal}. The practical line includes the integration of handlers into OCaml~\cite{sivaramakrishnan2021retrofitting} and efficient compilation schemes based on evidence passing~\cite{xie2021generalized}. Particularly relevant to our work are systems in which effects are represented as capabilities: Effekt introduces a type discipline for scope-based and type-based reasoning~\cite{brachthauser2020effects,brachthauser2022effects}, while capture tracking refines function types with sets of captured values~\cite{boruch2023capturing}. $Core_\Delta$ uses coarser but more compact information: a value is either free to escape outward or locally confined.

Mechanization of the metatheory of type systems builds on the experience of POPLMark and subsequent work on the engineering of formal metatheory~\cite{aydemir2008engineering}. The tools Ott and Autosubst show that working with syntax and substitutions can be automated~\cite{sewell2010ott,schaefer2015autosubst,stark2019autosubst2}. In our artifact a more direct variant is chosen: pure de Bruijn indices and recursive shift and substitution functions. This makes the definitions transparent and portable, but leaves four substitution lemmas as explicit technical obligations.

This survey yields the requirements for the proposed model. It must: (i) describe lifetimes in types without overloading the user with parameters; (ii) confine a runtime capability within its handler; (iii) admit first-class continuations yet not let them escape; (iv) have a checkable metatheory suitable as a specification for a compiler implementation.

\section{A Calculus with Existential Lifetimes}\label{sec:calculus}
Lifetimes are given by the grammar
\[
\Delta ::= l \vor free \vor local \vor \Delta_1 + \Delta_2 .
\]
The operation $+$ is interpreted as a minimum: a value built from several fields is safe no longer than its shortest-lived field. Types include variables, functions, data constructors, and two kinds of polymorphism:
\[
\tau ::= \alpha \vor \overline{\tau}\,\Delta \to \sigma
\vor K\,\Delta\,\overline{\tau}
\vor \forall l.\tau
\vor \forall(\alpha<:\tau').\tau .
\]
The annotation $K\,\Delta\,\overline{\tau}$ means that a value of constructor $K$ may be used for the lifetime $\Delta$. A key restriction applies to functions: the result must not contain a top-level $local$. Thanks to this, a local value cannot be returned across an ordinary function boundary.

Data constructors have no user-level lifetime parameters. If a constructor receives fields with lifetimes $\Delta_1,\ldots,\Delta_n$, the lifetime of the result equals $\Delta_1+\cdots+\Delta_n$. On pattern matching these forgotten lifetimes are recovered as fresh existential variables bounded by the lifetime of the scrutinized value. Variance-aware elimination replaces positive occurrences by the bound, negative ones by $free$, and rejects invariant positions. It is precisely this rule that forbids the classical leakage channels through containers and functions.

An effect handler is represented as
\[
\keyword{handle}\ E\ \overline{\tau}\ t_{op}\ \keyword{in}\ t,
\]
where $t_{op}$ is the body of the operation. On entering the handler a fresh runtime marker $m$ and a value $\keyword{cap}_E^m$ are created and substituted into the body. The call
\[
\keyword{perform}\ c\ \overline{\tau}\ v
\]
finds the nearest compatible handler, captures the pure evaluation context up to its delimiter, and passes the argument $v$ and the continuation $\keyword{resume}_m$ to the body of the operation. The semantics is deep: applying the continuation re-installs the delimiter of the same marker. This matches the standard behavior of Eff/Koka/Effekt handlers but is formalized without named marker variables: a marker does not bind program variables and therefore does not complicate substitution.

Two invariants are used for the safety of handlers. The first is type-based: the capability and the resumption have lifetime $local$ and hence cannot be returned outward through a function. The second is operational: the predicate $\keyword{marker\_ok}\ ms\ t$ records that every runtime capability with marker $m$ is located under the corresponding $\keyword{handler}_m$ or within the permitted active set $ms$. Source programs satisfy $\keyword{marker\_ok}\ [\,]\ t$.

The first main result is progress. If $\Gamma$ is a computation context, $\Gamma\vdash t:\tau$ and $\keyword{marker\_ok}\ [\,]\ t$, then $t$ is either a value or there exists $t'$ such that $t\step t'$. The proof uses a stronger open form: for a nonempty active set of markers a third alternative is allowed, a pending \texttt{perform} call. When the set is empty this alternative is impossible.

The second result is type preservation: if $\Gamma\vdash t:\tau$ and $t\step t'$, then $\Gamma\vdash t':\tau$. The ordinary reduction cases use the corresponding substitution lemmas. Non-typed runtime forms do not appear from source code arbitrarily: they are created only by the handler rule and immediately receive capability or resumption types with lifetime $local$.

Progress and preservation together yield type safety: a well-typed source program satisfying the marker invariant does not reach a stuck configuration. This property is important but insufficient for escape analysis. Therefore two specialized guarantees are additionally proved. The confinement theorem states that a runtime capability $\keyword{cap}_E^m$ cannot occur in a pure evaluation position outside its $\keyword{handler}_m$. The non-escape theorem states that if a computation of type $K\,free\,\overline{\sigma}$ terminates with a constructor value, then the top-level lifetime of that value is not $local$. Thus the type information indeed forbids the observable leakage of local values.

\section{Evaluation and Comparison}\label{sec:evaluation}
The evaluation of the artifact consists of three parts. The first is reproducibility: the verified core builds with standard Rocq/Coq without external libraries beyond the Stdlib. The build command generates a makefile from \texttt{src/\_CoqProject} and compiles the core; \texttt{Print Assumptions} on the top-level theorems shows only the substitution obligations of Table~\ref{tab:axioms}. The second part is example coverage. The modules \texttt{Examples.v} and \texttt{ExamplesProofs.v} contain positive $Core_\Delta$ programs, reader, state, exception, and optionality handlers, as well as step-by-step reduction traces. The third part is negative witnesses: checkable conditions reject programs in which a local capability is stored in a free state, in which \texttt{Option}\,$local$ is exposed through a free interface, or in which an existential lifetime is used contravariantly.

A comparison with related work is given in Table~\ref{tab:comparison}. Unlike Rust and OCaml \texttt{local}, our model includes first-class effect handlers; unlike Effekt and capture tracking, it uses an explicit lifetime minimum instead of capture sets. Modern handler implementations and compilation schemes~\cite{sivaramakrishnan2021retrofitting,xie2021generalized} demonstrate the practical significance of delimited control but do not address the problem of existential lifetimes in compound data.

A practical usage scenario for the artifact is the design of a compiler intermediate language in which stack or region allocation must coexist with capability-safe effect handlers. The typing rules specify local checks at function boundaries, and the operational semantics shows which runtime invariant is needed for a correct implementation of capture/resume. This makes the formalization not only a proof of a property of a particular calculus, but also a checkable reference for the design of languages with safe lifetime management.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}\label{sec:discussion}
The main limitation of the current version is the four substitution lemmas left as technical obligations. They do not change the rules of the language but must be completed for a fully self-contained proof artifact. The second limitation is single-operation effects. Multi-operation effects are encoded by a sum-typed argument, but the user-level notation becomes cumbersome; the natural development is to add operation tags. The third limitation is the conservative typing of operation arguments: in the current variant local values may enter the handler body through the capability and the resumption, but the operation argument is restricted to a free type. Extending this to ``local in, never out'' would require threading the lifetime of the argument through the continuation type and the handler's answer.

Another direction is a certified type-checking algorithm. At present the Rocq development specifies the typing and subtyping relations, while a prototype checking algorithm is maintained separately. Extracting a reference checker from the proven definitions would shorten the distance between the metatheory and a compiler implementation. Finally, it is useful to compare the chosen de Bruijn representation with automated infrastructures: Autosubst may reduce the amount of routine, but it is important to preserve the explicit connection between lifetime substitution and the handler rules.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
We have presented a machine-checked metatheory of the $Core_\Delta$ calculus extended with single-operation algebraic effect handlers. The calculus combines existential lifetimes in data, an explicit lifetime minimum, bounded polymorphism, and a capability-passing semantics of handlers. In Rocq/Coq we mechanized the syntax, subtyping, typing, small-step semantics, canonical forms, progress, preservation, type safety, and two specialized guarantees of escape analysis. Modulo four standard substitution lemmas, it is proved that a well-typed program does not get stuck, a runtime capability does not escape the scope of its handler, and a value of a free data type does not hide a top-level $local$. The example library exercises the model on reader/state/exception/optionality handlers and on programs that the analysis must reject.

The resulting artifact is useful as a specification for compilers of languages in which safe stack allocation, resource ownership, and first-class effect handlers must work within a single type system. Future work consists of completing the substitution metatheory, extending effects to multi-operation declarations, and extracting a certified checking algorithm.
